# Minnesota Rural Healthcare Under Threat — Data-Driven Advocacy Playbook for One-Person Marketing Teams

## Purpose

Rural healthcare facilities across Minnesota face an existential crisis. Federal Medicaid and Medicare payment delays, proposed cuts, and potential political retaliation against the state threaten to close the doors of hospitals and clinics that are the sole source of care for hundreds of thousands of Minnesotans. Many of the communities that depend on these facilities voted for the administration now proposing these cuts — and they will be the ones left in healthcare deserts when the doors close.

This report arms the solo marketer at a rural hospital or clinic with the data they need to make the case for their facility's survival — to community members, local elected officials, state legislators, and anyone who will listen. This is not patient acquisition marketing. This is advocacy marketing. The audience isn't captured — it's at risk of having nowhere to go.

**Data sources:** USDA RUCA codes, CMS hospital/clinic enrollment data, and HRSA shortage area designations, accessed via Mimi Labs (March 2026).

## 1. Understanding RUCA Codes (Rural-Urban Commuting Area)

All of the data in this report uses RUCA codes — the USDA's classification system for measuring how "rural" a ZIP code is. Based on population density and daily commuting patterns, they tell you not just where people live, but where they travel for work and services.

| RUCA Code | Classification | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| **1–3** | **Metro** | Urban cores and suburbs. Dense healthcare access, multiple competing facilities. |
| **4–6** | **Micropolitan / Large Town** | Small cities (10,000–49,999 pop). Some hospital infrastructure but may lack specialists. |
| **7–9** | **Small Town** | Towns under 10,000. Residents commute to larger areas for many services. |
| **10** | **Rural** | Most rural. Low density, long distances, often only one facility (if any) within a reasonable drive. |

**Why RUCA 10 matters most:** A hospital in a RUCA 10 ZIP code isn't competing for patients — it's the only option. When that facility loses funding, there is no alternative. The nearest ER could be 45+ minutes away. RUCA codes turn "rural" from a vague label into a measurable, mappable, policy-relevant classification — and a powerful data point for advocacy.

## 3. The Facilities on the Line

### 60+ Critical Access Hospitals Across Rural Minnesota

Critical Access Hospitals (CAHs) are 25-bed-or-fewer facilities that exist specifically because they are the only hospital within a reasonable distance. They receive cost-based Medicare reimbursement because the federal government itself recognized that normal payment rates would make them financially unviable. **Cutting or delaying those payments doesn't just hurt these hospitals — it eliminates the very mechanism designed to keep them open.**

| Rurality | Hospitals | Critical Access | Emergency Services | Avg Star Rating |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rural** | 56 | Majority | Nearly all | 4.0 |
| **Small Town** | 17 | Many | Most | 3.6 |
| **Micropolitan** | 29 | Several | Most | 3.8 |

These aren't failing hospitals. **Rural MN hospitals average a 4.0 CMS star rating** — higher than micropolitan and small town facilities. They are delivering quality care. The threat isn't performance. It's funding.

### 48 Rural Health Clinic Organizations Across 97 Towns

Beyond hospitals, **48 RHC organizations operate 94 clinic locations across 97 Minnesota towns**. These are the primary care front door — the place where someone gets their diabetes managed, their blood pressure checked, their child's ear infection treated. Many operate under system brands (Essentia, Sanford, CentraCare) but serve hyper-local communities. When a clinic closes in a town of 2,000 people, there is no Urgent Care down the street.

## 5. The Advocacy Marketing Playbook

This is what a solo marketer at a rural MN hospital needs to do **right now** — and what Mason Jar Marketing is uniquely positioned to help them execute.

### Strategy 1: Make It Local and Personal — "If This Hospital Closes"

**The data to use:** RUCA codes + Hospital General data

Every community member knows their hospital. What they may not know is what happens without it. The marketer's job is to make the abstract threat concrete:

- **"Your nearest alternative ER is 47 miles away."** Use RUCA data and facility locations to calculate the actual distance to the next nearest facility. Put that number on every piece of communication.
- **"This hospital employs 215 people in a county of 5,000."** Rural hospitals are often the #1 or #2 employer. Closure isn't just a healthcare issue — it's an economic collapse.
- **"Last year, our ER treated X emergencies. Where do those patients go?"** Pull from facility data to make the volume real.

**Deliverables Mason Jar could create:**
- One-page community fact sheet (print + digital) customized per facility
- Social media templates with local stats baked in
- Letter-to-the-editor templates for community members
- Infographic: "What happens when your hospital closes"

### Strategy 2: Arm the Community to Advocate — "Here's Who to Call"

**The data to use:** HPSA designations + facility data

The solo marketer can't lobby. But they can equip their community to speak up. This is communications work, not political work.

- **Create a simple "Take Action" page** on the hospital website with contact info for MN's congressional delegation, state legislators, and county commissioners
- **Draft template letters** that community members can personalize — focused on the specific impact to their community, using the HPSA and facility data
- **Organize community listening sessions** and provide the data that frames the conversation: "Here are the facts about what our facility means to this county"

**Key message framework:**
> "Our hospital serves [X] people across [Y] square miles. We are a federally designated Health Professional Shortage Area with a severity score of [Z]. Delayed Medicare/Medicaid payments put our facility at risk of closure. The nearest alternative is [N] miles away. We need [specific ask]."

### Strategy 3: Tell the Story Before It's Too Late — "We Are Still Here"

**The data to use:** Hospital star ratings + community context

Rural MN hospitals are delivering 4-star care on shoestring budgets. That story isn't being told. The national narrative is about rural healthcare decline. The local narrative should be about resilience — and the specific, named threat to that resilience.

- **"4 Stars and Fighting for Our Lives"** — Campaign concept that pairs quality data with the funding crisis. "We earned a 4-star CMS rating. We're delivering excellent care. And we may have to close because of federal payment delays."
- **Staff spotlight series** — Put faces on the threat. The nurse who drives 25 minutes to work at the only hospital in the county. The lab tech who grew up in the community.
- **Patient story collection** — "This hospital saved my life" campaigns that build community ownership of the facility

### Strategy 4: Build a Coalition — "Every Rural Hospital in MN Is in This Together"

**The data to use:** Full list of 60+ CAHs + 48 RHC organizations

No single facility has the marketing budget or political weight to fight this alone. But 60+ Critical Access Hospitals and 48 RHC organizations acting together is a different story.

- **Coordinate messaging across facilities** — Consistent talking points, shared data, unified campaign identity
- **"Rural MN Healthcare" brand** — A shared advocacy identity that any facility can use alongside their own brand
- **Joint letters and joint media** — A letter signed by 60 hospital CEOs hits differently than one from a single facility
- **Shared social media campaign** — A hashtag, a visual identity, a day of action

**This is where Mason Jar's "brand standards + single point of contact" model becomes invaluable.** A coalition of 60 under-resourced facilities needs exactly what Mason Jar offers: someone who can create consistent, professional communications across many organizations without big-agency overhead.

### Strategy 5: Target the Irony — With Compassion, Not Blame

**The data to use:** RUCA codes + HPSA + political context

Many of the communities most affected voted for the administration proposing these cuts. This is a delicate reality, but it's also a powerful narrative when handled with care:

- **Frame it as "we supported this administration AND we need them to support us back"** — not betrayal, but accountability
- **"We're not asking for a favor — we're asking to keep what was promised"** — Medicare/Medicaid aren't handouts; they're programs these communities paid into
- **"This isn't partisan — this is survival"** — The closing of a rural hospital hurts every resident regardless of how they voted
- **Amplify local voices** — Let community members tell their own story. A farmer in Renville County explaining what it means to lose the local clinic is more powerful than any campaign
